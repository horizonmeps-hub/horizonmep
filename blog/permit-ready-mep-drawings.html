What Makes an MEP Drawing Permit-Ready?

Engineering Insights • Permitting & Compliance

Permit delays are one of the most common reasons construction schedules slip. In many cases, the issue isn’t architectural scope — it’s incomplete or poorly coordinated MEP drawings.

So what does “permit-ready” actually mean from an Authority Having Jurisdiction (AHJ) perspective?

1. Code Compliance Is Clearly Documented

AHJs expect MEP drawings to explicitly demonstrate compliance with adopted codes such as IMC, IPC, NEC, NFPA, and local amendments. Missing references, unclear calculations, or generic notes often trigger review comments.

2. Load Calculations Are Included and Consistent

HVAC, electrical, and plumbing load calculations must align with equipment selections and schedules. Inconsistent values across drawings raise red flags during plan review.

3. Systems Are Coordinated Across Disciplines

Permit reviewers routinely flag conflicts between mechanical, electrical, plumbing, and fire protection systems. Coordinated drawings demonstrate engineering diligence and reduce RFIs during construction.

4. Life Safety Systems Are Fully Addressed

Fire alarm, sprinkler layouts, and egress-related systems must comply with NFPA standards and local fire department requirements. Missing life-safety details almost always delay approval.

Engineering Insight
Permit-ready drawings are not “minimum sets.” They are coordinated, reviewed, and structured to answer AHJ questions before they’re asked.
